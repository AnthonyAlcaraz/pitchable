I spent 11 hours building a pitch deck that got dismissed in under 4 minutes.

The prospect glanced at it, said "looks good," and asked me to just send the summary in an email.

That was the moment I realized: the deck isn't the problem. The time it takes to build the deck is.

So I built Pitchable.

You drop in a brief, paste a document, or type a topic. You pick the audience, goal, tone, and narrative framework. Pitchable generates a structured, styled, exportable deck in 2 to 15 minutes.

Then you edit via chat. "Make slide 3 shorter." "Add a market sizing breakdown." "Rewrite the CTA as a question." No slide editor. No PowerPoint formatting spiral.

After 6 months of building:
— 35 users in production
— 182 presentations generated
— 377 chat messages used to iterate on decks
— 143 successful exports to PPTX or PDF

The number I care most about is 377. Users don't just generate once and leave. They come back and iterate. That's the signal that the editing loop actually works.

Free tier: 15 credits, no card, 2–3 complete decks.

If you build decks — for investors, for clients, for internal reviews — I'd love your honest feedback.

## Carousel: "10 Things I Learned Building an AI Pitch Deck Tool"

**Format:** LinkedIn carousel, 10 slides. Each slide = one lesson, one number or concrete detail. No corporate speak.

---

### Slide 1 — Cover

**Headline:** 10 Things I Learned Building an AI Pitch Deck Tool

**Subtext:** 182 presentations. 35 users. 6 months. Here's what the data actually showed.

**Visual:** Dark card, white text, Pitchable logo bottom-right.

---

### Slide 2 — Lesson 1

**Headline:** Users don't want AI to write their deck. They want AI to structure it.

**Body:** Every user who churned early said the same thing: "It doesn't sound like me." The ones who stayed used the chat editor to rewrite slides in their own voice. The generation is scaffolding, not the final product.

---

### Slide 3 — Lesson 2

**Headline:** "Time to first deck" is the only metric that matters for activation.

**Body:** If a user doesn't see a generated deck in their first session, they don't come back. 2–15 minutes is the range. The users who got a deck in under 5 minutes had 3x higher return rates than those who waited 15.

---

### Slide 4 — Lesson 3

**Headline:** Consultants iterate more than founders.

**Body:** I built Pitchable for startup founders raising rounds. Turns out management consultants use the chat editor twice as often per session. They have more decks to build and a higher tolerance for iterating on structure. Lesson: ship for your actual users, not your assumed ones.

---

### Slide 5 — Lesson 4

**Headline:** The chat editor is more valuable than the generator.

**Body:** 377 chat messages across 182 decks means an average of 2 edits per deck. Some users sent 15+ messages on a single deck. The generation gets them in the door. The chat keeps them there.

---

### Slide 6 — Lesson 5

**Headline:** Hallucinations in market sizing are the #1 support complaint.

**Body:** Claude invents market size numbers. I knew this would happen and shipped anyway with a "verify this data" warning. Still got 4 complaints in the first week. The fix isn't better prompting — it's grounding generation in user-provided sources. That's the next feature.

---

### Slide 7 — Lesson 6

**Headline:** Free tier converts better when framed as "decks," not "credits."

**Body:** "15 credits free" confused people. "2–3 complete decks free" converted at roughly double the rate on the same page. Features are for engineers. Outcomes are for users.

---

### Slide 8 — Lesson 7

**Headline:** Document upload is underused and undervalued.

**Body:** Only 30% of users upload a document. But those who do produce better decks and edit less. Extraction anchors the AI to real content instead of invented content. I need to push this feature harder in onboarding.

---

### Slide 9 — Lesson 8

**Headline:** Marp CLI was the right rendering choice, but nobody cares.

**Body:** I chose Marp CLI for slide rendering because it's open-source, reliable, and lets LLMs edit slides as Markdown. Technical audiences love it. Everyone else just wants a nice-looking deck. Right tool, wrong marketing angle for most users.

---

### Slide 10 — Lesson 9

**Headline:** Shipping early surfaces things you can't anticipate.

**Body:** I thought the bottleneck was generation quality. It was actually the status indicator during generation. Users assumed it was broken after 90 seconds of silence. A progress log showing "Rendering slide 4 of 12..." fixed a problem I didn't know existed.

---

### Slide 11 — Closing Slide

**Headline:** Lesson 10: The deck that gets built always beats the perfect deck in someone's head.

**Body:** 182 decks created. 143 exported. 35 users. None of them waited until it was perfect.
